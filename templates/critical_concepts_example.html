Both Micah and his friend Kyle are high school seniors who applied to colleges as pre-med majors. Both are deciding between two very different schools. Micah is interested in academic medicine and has already spent a summer in a lab. He imagines himself pursuing a fellowship or additional research training even after medical school and residency. He's deciding between the University of Pittsburgh, where he's been offered a full scholarship, and Johns Hopkins University, which has not offered his family any financial assistance.

However, for Micah, an only child of two professional parents, the sizable cost difference between his two choices is not his primary Critical Concepts. Micah and his parents are concerned with providing him with an undergraduate education that will set him up for the specific kind of medical career he envisioned. Equally important Critical Concepts for Micah are the accessibility of undergraduate research opportunities, medical school acceptance rates, and the selectivity of the medical schools each undergraduate institution is getting their students into.

Kyle, on the other hand, is one of four children, also deciding between the University of Pittsburgh, with a free ride, and Johns Hopkins, with some aid. Kyle is pretty sure he wants to be a family practice doctor, and has no interest in research. Kyle's parents have saved $40,000 dollars towards his education, but any additional costs will be his responsibility. For Kyle, the cost of his undergraduate options is his overriding Critical Concept.
