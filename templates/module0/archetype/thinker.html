{% extends 'modules/base/archetype.html' %} {% load module_tags static %} {% block strength_text %} Being the Thinker means that you will never get caught off guard by the consequences of the decisions you make. You are extremely thorough and thoughtful. You are cautious—you know what warning signs to look for, and you are good at steering yourself away from them. You're a master at making pro and con lists, and you rarely make impulse decisions. This is a powerful thing, because you always know what you're up against. It's hard to trick you, because you never act without considering every possible angle. {% endblock %} {% block strength_list %}
  • Thorough: Tends to think things through
  • Thoughtful: Tends to considers options
  • Cautious: Tends to be careful and measured
    • {% endblock %} {% block blind_spot_text %} Being a Thinker can have its negatives, too. Sometimes, because you are so cautious, you pick the safe option so that you don't end up failing. This "loss aversion" means that you lose out on the opportunity to fail, and learn from your failures, which is proven to help you grow twice as fast as success does. Because you are a master at comparing and weighing your options, you sometimes don’t weigh the value of each option individually. For example, option 1 may cost less than option 2, but if they are both under your budget, there's no need to eliminate the more expensive option. This "relativity bias" keeps you from seeing things as they actually are, and forces you to see them in comparison to something else. And finally, because you are committed to thoughtfully looking at what the "best" option is, you have the tendency to believe what authority figures say and could fall prey to “authority bias.” If someone you admire suggests a decision path, you'll give that choice more weight than you should. {% endblock %} {% block blind_spot_list %}
  • Loss Aversion: Tendency to prefer avoiding losses to acquiring equivalent gains: it is better to not lose $5 than to find $5
  • Relativity Bias: Tendency to make relative, rather than absolute evaluations
  • Authority Bias: Tendency to attribute greater accuracy to the opinion of an authority figure
